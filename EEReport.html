Impact of Crosshair Colour on Aiming Accuracy in VALORANT

3rd Year, Semester 1 University Assignment
November 2024 - January 2025

Abstract
In first-person shooter (FPS) games, crosshair design plays a pivotal role in aiming accuracy and overall performance. This study examines how different crosshair colours affect player’s accuracy in VALORANT, aiming to identify the optimal colour choice that enhances accuracy while aiming.

For this study, I asked 27 participants to complete a series of standardised tasks, using four distinct crosshair colours; white, green, yellow, and red. Each colour crosshair was used three times, and accuracy was recorded, along with an average accuracy. A within-subject analysis was conducted, comparing each participant’s average accuracy with each crosshair colour against their overall average performance. This data was then also sorted into participant skill level, allowing for a deeper analysis.

Quantitative analysis showed that participants using the green crosshair demonstrated a statistically significant improvement in accuracy compared to other colors (p < 0.05). Conversely, the yellow crosshair resulted in the poorest performance across all skill levels, likely due to its lack of contrast against the in-game environment. Red and white crosshairs showed mixed results, with red slightly outperforming white at mid-skill levels but showing no significant advantage overall. Qualitative survey responses supported these findings, with participants rating green as the easiest to see and yellow as the most distracting. Visibility and player preference were closely aligned with performance metrics, further reinforcing the importance of contrast and visibility in crosshair design.

Introduction
The field of Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) seeks to enhance the user experience through an understanding of how individuals interact with digital systems. Within the context of first-person shooter (FPS) games, such as Riot Games' VALORANT, the design and usability of interface elements play a crucial role in influencing player performance and satisfaction. This study explores the impact of crosshair color on player accuracy and effectiveness within the FPS genre, focusing on its potential to enhance the user experience by optimizing visual clarity and reducing cognitive load.

FPS games rely heavily on precision, reaction time, and environmental awareness, making the crosshair a vital interface element. The crosshair serves as the player's primary tool for aiming and targeting, and its design can significantly affect gameplay performance. While existing literature in game design and HCI highlights the importance of interface customization (Dye et al., 2009; Johnson & Wiles, 2003), limited research has examined the nuanced role of crosshair aesthetics, particularly color, in FPS games.

To investigate this, the study utilizes VALORANT's Practice Range—a controlled environment where variables such as enemy behavior and map layout remain consistent—to test the effects of crosshair color on accuracy. Participants are asked to complete standardized drills within the Practice Range using different crosshair colors, with data collected on their performance metrics, such as shot accuracy and target acquisition times.

Method
This section highlights the methods used during this study. It follows a clear and precise layout, allowing the study to be replicated by any future researchers.

Researcher Preparation
Before recruiting any participants, there are a few tasks that need to be completed by the researcher, including; ethical checklist, information sheet, consent forms, instruction document, score document, and survey.

Although ethics checklists are unique to a university environment, it is crucial to still take into consideration, to protect both participants and the researcher.

The information sheet, which is provided to participants, explains the details of the project, and ethical considerations, such as data collection and how data will be stored. This document also included all points of contact both with the researcher and, in this case, the university and module supervisor.

The consent form is an agreement between the participant and the researcher. The participants sign the form to agree that they understand; the purpose of the project, they agree to take part, their right to withdraw, their participation is anonymous, and they are over 18. They are also given the opportunity to provide an email address to be informed of the project outcomes.

I then made up a document providing instructions for the specific tasks which the participants were asked to complete for the project. The purpose of this document is to allow the participant to complete the tasks online and in their own time, giving specific instructions for them to follow without the need for the researcher to be present.

A separate document was also made up, allowing users to enter the scores they obtained whilst doing the tasks. This allowed the participant to see clearly where they needed to enter their data, but also allowed the researcher to have a standardised layout across all participants. Score table.


Finally, a survey was made in order to collect some qualitative data from the participants. This included questions such as; VALORANT rank, which crosshair was easiest to see, which crosshair was hardest to see, if any colours were distracting, and which crosshair they preferred aiming with.

The information sheet, instructions, consent form, and score document are all put into a google folder. This folder can be duplicated for each participant.

Recruiting Participants
Participants were recruited primarily through discord. A wide variety of skill levels across VALORANT were seeked out, ranging from iron to immortal. I didn’t take any demographic data throughout the entire project so age and gender amongst participants wasn’t known, except that everyone was over 18.

After expressing interest, each participant was given their own google folder (containing the information sheet, instructions, consent form and score document), along with a random 3 digit number - their unique participant number. This number was randomly generated each time using an online number generator. It allowed the participants' data to remain anonymous.

Participants were recruited over a two week period, depending on when they expressed interest. Each participant was given notice of a deadline, which ended roughly 2 weeks after the last participant was recruited. The participants were left to complete the tasks in their own time, but they were free to message or email the recruiter with any questions.

Results
This section presents the findings on how different crosshair colours affect player accuracy in VALORANT. Firstly, the quantitative data is discussed; showing the raw data, how it was analysed, and the significance of those results. Followed by the qualitative data and its meaning.

Raw Quantitative Data
Raw data from each participant was taken from their score tables and placed into a Google Sheets spreadsheet. For each crosshair colour an average of their own scores was recorded, along with their overall average performance. An “average difference” was then calculated for each colour by minusing their overall average from their colour specific average. An example of one section of the table is shown below:

Screenshot of the white crosshair average score table.


Using the participant numbers; ranks were assigned to the scores (participant ranks were provided in the survey) and they were grouped by rank. An overall average difference for each crosshair colour, for each rank was calculated by taking an average of all the participants within that group. Below shows a table of the data and a visual bar chart:

Table showing: average scores for each crosshair, in each VALORANT rank.

Graph showing: average scores for each crosshair, in each VALORANT rank.


From this raw data alone, we can deduce that the yellow crosshairs overall had a negative impact on the accuracy of the participants, whereas the green appears to have a positive effect. The red crosshair had an effect amongst the middle ranks, but at the higher and lower ends of the scale had very little effect on accuracy. The white crosshair seemed to have the smallest effect overall of all the colours. These results are taken from just the raw data and we cannot know for sure if any of the colours had a significant effect without conducting a hypothesis test, results of this are discussed in the next section.

Analysis of Quantitative Data
From the raw data we could see a pattern that the yellow crosshair was having a negative impact on the performance of the participants. However, there were no calculations to be sure that it was the crosshair causing the decrease in performance and it wasn’t down to chance.

In order to justify if the crosshair was causing a significant performance difference, I used a null hypothesis test, with a significance level of 5% (or 0.05). The overall average difference for all the ranks was taken, and each crosshair colour was compared with all the others. The results of this can be seen in the table below:

Table showing hypothesis test values for each colour crosshair.


A P-value of 0.05 and greater indicates that there is no significant difference between the participants' performance with the crosshair colours. This is indicated by the red highlighting between the red and white, and red and green crosshairs. A P-value lower than 0.05 indicates that there is a significant difference between the performance of the colours. This is indicated by the green highlighting on the table.

This data supports the conclusion that the yellow crosshair was having a negative impact on performance. The yellow crosshair had a p-value lower than the significance level when compared to each of the other colours, meaning there was a significant difference in the performance levels.

The red crosshair seemed to have the least effect on performance, with only the red vs yellow crosshairs having a significant difference. Whilst the green and white crosshairs both had a slight impact, we know from the data that the green gave a slight positive effect, and the white gave a slight negative effect.

Discussion and Conclusion
This study highlights the significant impact of crosshair color on aiming accuracy in Valorant. Green crosshairs, with their high contrast and visibility, emerged as the most effective choice for improving performance, while yellow crosshairs hindered accuracy due to their poor contrast with the in-game environment. These results underline the importance of customizable crosshair options in FPS games and suggest that players and developers should prioritize visibility and environmental contrast when designing crosshair elements.

The findings also underscore the interconnectedness of quantitative and qualitative data in understanding user preferences and performance. For instance, survey feedback indicated that visibility and ease of use directly correlated with performance outcomes, reinforcing the importance of player-centered design in game interfaces. The alignment between participants' subjective preferences and their objective performance outcomes further validates the study’s methodology and results.

While this research offers valuable insights, it is not without limitations. The controlled environment of Valorant's Practice Range may not fully replicate competitive gameplay conditions, where additional variables such as stress and dynamic environments come into play. Future research could expand on this work by investigating crosshair performance in live matches or exploring additional customization options, such as adaptive crosshairs that change color based on the environment.

Ultimately, this study contributes to the field of HCI by emphasizing the value of user-driven design in gaming. By prioritizing visibility and usability, game developers can create more engaging and accessible experiences, enhancing both casual and competitive play.
