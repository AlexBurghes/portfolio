Built to Break: How Failure Shapes Player Experience

3rd Year, Semester 2 University Assignment
April 2025 - May 2025

Introduction
Failure is often seen as the opposite of success, a negative outcome to be avoided. However, in the context of video games, failure is not only expected but deliberately designed. It serves as a mechanic that shapes player experience, emotional investment, and long-term engagement. In games, failure can take many forms: mechanical, narrative, social, or philosophical, and each type influences how players interact with the game’s systems. This essay defines failure as any disruption of the player's intended outcome, whether through loss, rejection of agency, or unmet expectations. Unlike other media, games require active participation, which means that failure becomes a functional part of the experience rather than a narrative endpoint. The way games balance frustration and reward through failure is central to their emotional and psychological appeal.

Videogames balance frustration and reward by transforming failure into engagement. This essay examines narrative failure in The Stanley Parable and competitive mechanical failure in VALORANT to explore how games manipulate player psychology to sustain motivation despite, or because of: failure. While these games belong to radically different genres, they both use failure as a tool for player immersion and reflection. The following sections will establish a theoretical framework for understanding failure in games, before analysing how each case study constructs and deploys failure. A comparative analysis will then show how frustration can be used to shape emotional and psychological responses across different game types, revealing failure as a sophisticated and versatile design strategy.

The Paradox of Failure
Jesper Juul identifies a central paradox in game design: “players want to feel competent, yet they choose to play games that make them feel incompetent” (Juul, 2013). This contradiction lies at the heart of why failure is not only tolerated in games but actively sought. Unlike traditional media, games challenge the player’s agency and ability, meaning that failure isn’t a byproduct; it’s a design goal. A game without the possibility of failure cannot truly challenge the player, and thus cannot offer a satisfying sense of accomplishment. Failure, then, is not a flaw in design but a fundamental component of engagement.

Katherine Isbister adds an emotional dimension to this discussion, arguing that failure in games is not purely mechanical but also deeply affective. It can evoke emotions such as: shame, pride, anticipation, and relief, making the player’s experience more immersive and personal (Isbister, 2017). These emotional responses are designed, not incidental. By creating moments of tension and frustration, developers give players a reason to care. Emotional investment increases when the player feels they have something at stake, and it is often the threat or reality of failure that generates those stakes. As such, failure plays a crucial role in shaping emotional intensity in gameplay.

Challenge, Flow, and Learning
The psychological underpinning of failure’s effectiveness can be found in Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi’s concept of flow. Flow is a state of optimal experience achieved when a person’s skills are in perfect balance with a task’s challenges (Csikszentmihalyi, 1990). In the context of games, flow is central to sustained engagement. If the challenge is too low, players become bored; if it is too high, they become anxious or frustrated. Many games intentionally operate close to the upper edge of this threshold, pushing players toward failure in order to maintain engagement. The temporary discomfort of failure becomes motivation to improve and re-enter the flow state.

Raph Koster supports this view by suggesting that fun in games is primarily derived from learning. In his view, “games are fun because they teach us things in a very fundamental way” (Koster, 2013). Failure is not a barrier to enjoyment but a signal that there is something new to master. Through repeated attempts, players learn patterns and adjust strategies. This loop of trial, error, and improvement is what makes games both educational and enjoyable. Whether in a high-pressure tactical shooter like “VALORANT” or a narrative experiment like “The Stanley Parable”, failure facilitates progress and engagement.

Rules, Fiction, and Frustration
Jesper Juul’s theory of games as “half-real” further illuminates the experience of failure. Games are composed of two intertwined layers: the rules that govern mechanics and the fictional world that gives them context (Juul, 2005). Players are often willing to accept surreal or abstract narrative settings as long as the rules remain consistent. Fairness in rules helps justify failure, even when the story elements are strange or confusing. This duality allows players to invest in the challenge and make sense of their setbacks.

However, some games use this dual structure to create deliberate frustration. The Stanley Parable disrupts both its mechanical and fictional consistency, denying the player a sense of agency. It presents choices and then ridicules the player for making them, creating a sense of disorientation. Failure in this context is not about losing a game state but about failing to assert control or find meaning. This kind of narrative frustration shows how failure can serve a thematic function, raising questions about free will, authorship, and the role of the player within the game world.

Having established the emotional, cognitive, and theoretical dimensions of failure in games, the essay now turns to two case studies that handle failure and frustration in contrasting ways. The Stanley Parable uses failure to unsettle and reflect, challenging the very nature of player agency. VALORANT, by contrast, channels failure into mastery and competition, where each loss offers the potential for growth. Together, they demonstrate the flexibility and power of failure as a core game mechanic.

The Stanley Parable: Failure of Agency and Meaning
Introduction to The Stanley Parable
Released in 2013 by Galactic Cafe, The Stanley Parable is a narrative-driven exploration game that challenges conventional ideas of player choice and agency. Rather than relying on mechanical difficulty, the game is constructed around narrative subversion, where failure is not the result of poor performance but an inevitable part of the game’s design. Set in an empty office complex, players control Stanley, a silent office worker who discovers one day that all of his coworkers have vanished. As a disembodied narrator guides and comments on the player’s actions, the game explores themes of control, obedience, and the illusion of free will. The Stanley Parable is not about winning or mastering mechanics, it is about confronting the structures of meaning that games (and players) often take for granted. It reframes failure not as a setback but as the core of its narrative and philosophical engagement.

Constructing Failure Through Narrative
At its core, The Stanley Parable constructs failure through the deliberate breakdown of agency. While the game appears to offer choices: left or right, obey or disobey, the outcomes are tightly scripted, and the illusion of freedom is consistently undermined. Regardless of the path taken, the player cannot achieve a definitive victory. Each narrative branch loops back into another form of failure: either the story ends abruptly, Stanley dies, the world resets, or the narrator mocks the futility of the player’s decisions. This recursive structure exposes the artificiality of agency in games. By denying players the satisfaction of “winning”, The Stanley Parable forces them to confront how constrained their choices truly are.

This design exemplifies Jesper Juul’s paradox of failure. As Juul (2013) argues, players enter games seeking competence but are often drawn to experiences that repeatedly make them feel incompetent. In The Stanley Parable, this incompetence is not based on mechanical challenge, but on the player’s lack of narrative control. The game creates what might be called “structured incompetence”: a scenario where every option feels meaningless, yet the player continues to search for a better outcome. It frustrates the expectation that effort should lead to mastery, and replaces success with existential uncertainty. In doing so, it creates a space where failure becomes the dominant mode of interaction.

Subverting Game Logic and the Absurd
This rejection of conventional game logic is further illuminated by Espen Aarseth’s concept of ergodic literature. Aarseth (1997) defines ergodic texts as those which require nontrivial effort from the reader or player to traverse. In most games, this effort is rewarded with progression or success. The Stanley Parable subverts this model by requiring effort that yields no meaningful reward. The player explores, restarts, experiments, and is constantly looped back into failure. This inversion breaks the traditional link between input and outcome, reframing gameplay as a critique of interactive structure itself. The game becomes a commentary on the futility of seeking coherence or control in systems that are fundamentally pre-written.

This futility resonates with the philosophical framework of absurdism, particularly in the work of Albert Camus. Camus (1942) describes the absurd condition as the conflict between the human desire for meaning and the silent indifference of the universe. In The Stanley Parable, players are trapped in this absurd condition. They perform actions, follow paths, disobey, restart, and yet achieve nothing lasting. Like Sisyphus pushing his boulder uphill, the player is condemned to repetition without resolution. The game’s narrator mirrors this tension, by offering commentary that oscillates between humor, sarcasm, and despair. In embracing the absurd, the game uses failure as a form of philosophical engagement. The player is invited to find meaning not in outcomes, but in the act of playing itself.

Anti-Flow and Intentional Frustration
This approach also functions as a deliberate inversion of Csikszentmihalyi’s flow theory. As discussed earlier, flow is achieved when there is a balance between challenge and skill (Csikszentmihalyi, 1990). The Stanley Parable disrupts this entirely. There is no skill curve, no mastery, and no progression. Instead, players encounter constant narrative disruption, unpredictability, and emotional dissonance. The game creates intentional anti-flow: a state of unease and confusion that draws attention to the medium itself. Rather than immersing the player, it continually breaks immersion in order to provoke reflection. This is not an accident, but a core part of its design philosophy.

Player Impact and the Value of Failure
The emotional impact of this failure-first design is complex. Players may feel confused, frustrated, or amused, often within the same playthrough. The tone of the game, driven by the narrator’s voice, swings from dry wit to existential commentary. This unpredictability fosters a sense of critical distance. Rather than mastering a system, players are invited to question it. The reward becomes interpretive, not mechanical. Engagement is sustained not by flow or competition, but by curiosity; what else can the game do, how far can it go, what other endings might reveal new insights?

In this sense, The Stanley Parable offers a positive experience of failure. It reframes failure as an opportunity for reflection rather than a sign of inadequacy. The player’s inability to win becomes the very thing that makes the game compelling. This subversion of traditional gameplay expectations not only challenges how games are played but also how they are understood. Through narrative recursion, absurdist tone, and intentional frustration, The Stanley Parable turns failure into its most powerful design tool.

VALORANT: Competitive Failure and High-Stakes Reward
Introduction to VALORANT
Released in 2020 by Riot Games, VALORANT is a free-to-play tactical first-person shooter that places strong emphasis on precision, team coordination, and strategic planning. The game features round-based combat where players are divided into two teams, with one team attacking and the other defending. Each player selects an "agent", with unique abilities that complement traditional gunplay. Unlike narrative-driven games, VALORANT is designed around mechanical mastery and competitive play. Its high skill ceiling ensures that failure is not only frequent but also central to the experience. This case study explores how VALORANT incorporates failure through mechanical, social, and psychological systems, ultimately reinforcing long-term player engagement.

Systems of Failure in VALORANT
Failure in VALORANT occurs frequently and in multiple forms. Mechanical failure is the most immediate and visible. Players may miss a crucial shot, mismanage a one-versus-one encounter, or mis-time an ability, resulting in near-instant elimination from a round. The stakes of each interaction are high, and the margin for error is small. Unlike games that offer retries or checkpoints, VALORANT punishes mistakes with instant consequences. These mechanical failures accumulate and form a core part of the player’s learning curve.

Social and team-based failure adds another layer of complexity. Because VALORANT is a team game, individual performance is deeply interwoven with the actions of others. Poor communication or missed callouts can lead to a round loss. These losses often feel deeply personal, even if they are caused by others. This dynamic creates a psychological feedback loop where the player feels responsible not only for their own mistakes, but for the team’s overall performance.

Cumulative failure is another key element. VALORANT is structured in a series of rounds, and losing multiple rounds in succession can create emotional pressure and frustration. A bad start can snowball into a full match loss, and the stakes grow with each failed round. This accumulation of failure intensifies the emotional experience of gameplay, as the cost of each mistake compounds over time. As Juul (2013) explains, players pursue competence but are frequently met with micro-failures. In VALORANT, these micro-failures build a narrative of personal and team struggle, reinforcing the challenge and heightening the reward when success is finally achieved.

Theoretical Frameworks for Competitive Failure
Flow theory, as proposed by Csikszentmihalyi (1990), provides a useful framework for understanding player engagement in VALORANT. Flow occurs when there is a perfect balance between a player’s skill level and the challenge presented. However, this balance can easily be disrupted by poor matchmaking, team imbalance, or inconsistent performance. When the challenge becomes too difficult or easy, flow is broken, and the player experiences frustration or boredom. In VALORANT, flow is a fragile state that players continually chase. When achieved, it produces intense immersion, but when broken, it leads to sharp emotional dips. These fluctuations between flow and failure keep the player engaged, driven to improve and regain that balance.

B.F. Skinner’s operant conditioning, particularly the model of partial reinforcement, also plays a role in VALORANT’s design. According to this model, unpredictable and intermittent rewards are highly effective at sustaining behavior over time (Skinner, 1953). In VALORANT, success is not guaranteed. Players may experience long stretches of failure punctuated by small but meaningful victories—a well-timed clutch or a successful round. These small wins act as reinforcement, encouraging continued play despite frequent setbacks. This partial reinforcement system keeps the player hooked, as the next success always feels just within reach.

Loss aversion, a principle from behavioural economics introduced by Tversky and Kahneman (1991), further explains the emotional impact of failure in competitive games. Their research shows that people perceive losses more intensely than equivalent gains. In the context of VALORANT, this means that losing a match or a crucial round often feels more impactful than winning. The emotional weight of failure can lead to frustration, but it also raises the stakes of success. Because losses are felt so deeply, victories in turn become even more rewarding. The game amplifies emotional highs and lows, creating a compelling play experience.

Emotional and Motivational Impact on the Player
The emotional experience of failure in VALORANT is intense. Players often feel adrenaline, anxiety, anger, or even exhilaration during and after matches. The high-stakes nature of the game means that every decision, every shot, and every strategy carries significant weight. These emotions are not purely negative. They are part of what makes the game exciting and satisfying. Short-term frustration is balanced by long-term engagement. Players invest time not because the game is easy, but because improvement is visible and meaningful.

Over time, players develop goals related to skill improvement. They review mistakes, practice mechanics, and build communication strategies. Failure becomes a motivator for self-improvement. This makes success feel genuinely earned, particularly in high-pressure moments like clutch plays or match victories. These rare, rewarding moments are disproportionately powerful because they stand in contrast to the frequent failures that precede them. In this way, VALORANT turns competitive failure into a driver of engagement and mastery.

Comparative Analysis: Different Models of Balancing Frustration and Reward
Types of Failure: Narrative vs Competitive
The Stanley Parable and VALORANT both rely on failure as a central mechanic, but the nature of that failure differs fundamentally. In The Stanley Parable, failure is philosophical and narrative. The player encounters the illusion of agency, where every decision leads to an unsatisfying conclusion. Success is deliberately withheld, making failure a thematic and structural tool. In contrast, VALORANT centres on mechanical and competitive failure. Players miss shots, lose rounds, and are frequently outperformed by opponents. This failure is not abstract, but immediate and skill-based. While both games frustrate players, they do so through distinct systems that reflect their genre and goals. One undermines narrative expectation; the other challenges mechanical proficiency.

Emotional and Psychological Response to Failure
The emotional outcomes of failure in each game differ significantly. In The Stanley Parable, frustration provokes reflection. The lack of meaningful progress and the absurdity of the game’s logic encourages players to question their expectations of interactivity and narrative control. This leads to an emotional state rooted in curiosity, irony, or existential discomfort. By contrast, VALORANT channels frustration into motivation. Losses can be infuriating, but they often result in a drive to improve. Players analyse mistakes, train their aim, or work on communication, seeking redemption in the next match. In both cases, the games turn negative emotions into engagement, but they do so through different emotional trajectories: one through reflection, the other through competition and mastery.

Balancing Frustration and Reward in Game Design
Both games offer carefully crafted systems to manage frustration and sustain player interest. The Stanley Parable uses narrative loops, humour, and meta-commentary to soften the sting of failure. The narrator’s wit and the game’s unpredictable endings keep players intrigued, even when outcomes are intentionally unsatisfying. This turns failure into a playful experience. VALORANT, on the other hand, offsets frustration with partial rewards. Small victories, like winning a clutch round or landing a difficult shot, serve as emotional payoffs. The ranked progression system also gives players tangible markers of improvement, even in defeat. These design strategies reflect the demands of their respective genres. Narrative games reward persistence with insight; competitive games reward persistence with measurable growth. Both approaches show how frustration, when designed effectively, can become a powerful tool for sustained engagement.

Conclusion
Failure in video games is far more than an obstacle to success; it is a central mechanic through which games generate emotional intensity, cognitive engagement, and sustained motivation. By examining The Stanley Parable and VALORANT, this essay has demonstrated how games transform frustration into a meaningful part of the player experience. While the two case studies operate in different genres, both use failure to shape how players think and ultimately drive them to continue playing. In The Stanley Parable, failure is philosophical and narrative. The player is confronted with the illusion of choice and forced to reflect on the meaning of their actions. This disruption of agency leads to a form of engagement rooted in critical thought and curiosity. In contrast, VALORANT presents failure as a mechanical and competitive challenge. Players regularly lose rounds, make mistakes, and face the consequences of their actions in real time. Yet this consistent exposure to defeat becomes a motivating factor, encouraging players to improve and strive toward excellence.

The theoretical frameworks of Juul’s paradox of failure, Csikszentmihalyi’s flow theory, Aarseth’s ergodic literature, and psychological models such as operant conditioning and loss aversion reveal the complexity of failure in game design. Whether used to provoke existential reflection or competitive drive, failure becomes a tool that designers use to deepen emotional investment and encourage continued interaction. By manipulating how and why players fail, games transform what should be a deterrent into a source of meaning, challenge, and enjoyment. Failure, far from being a flaw in game design, is what makes games worth playing.
