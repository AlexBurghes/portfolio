The Subtle Power of Contemporary Media

2nd Year, Semester 1 University Assignment
November 2023

Introduction
Contemporary media operates as an omnipresent force, subtly weaving itself into the fabric of our daily lives. It silently shapes our thoughts, moulds our opinions and influences our behaviour, often without us realising it. Wendy Chun succinctly captures this paradoxical nature of media when she asserts, “Our media matter most when they seem not to matter at all” (2017). This quote encourages us to be critical consumers of media, and recognise the impact that it has goes beyond what we may initially perceive. In this essay, I will explore how contemporary media is integrated into our lives and, with its pervasive influence, how it institutes habits and addiction, reinforces stereotypes and has constructed a symbiotic relationship with society.

Habit and Addiction
The societal habits formed around media are continually erasing the distinction between routine behaviour and addictive tendencies. I believe we, as a society, are shifting from just using media, to relying on media in our daily lives. Wendy Chun (2017) highlights this interconnectedness of habit and addiction, asserting that "habit is becoming addiction: to have is to lose". This concept suggests that habitual behaviours can evolve into addictions, potentially resulting in personal loss or adverse outcomes. For instance, social media platforms foster a "social validation feedback loop" (Seymour, 2019), where users are motivated to seek more 'follows,' 'likes,' and 'shares' to gain validation and feel more compelled to engage with the platform. While individuals may initially use social media for communication with peers, for example, they often find themselves drawn into this feedback loop, transitioning from innocent routines to addictive behaviours. This underscores the pervasive influence of media in shaping and amplifying habitual behaviours.

Codependency
Technology is the backbone of contemporary society, our relationship with the digital world is so interwoven that we each rely on the other for growth and development. I believe that contemporary society is depending on technology for survival. Surry and Baker develop this idea by saying, “technology does not have to play the dominant role in human interaction in order to have a significant impact” (2015). This quote underscores the idea that technology's role in human interactions can be nuanced. It doesn't always have to be the central focus, but it can still have a significant and transformative impact on how it moulds society and how people engage with the world. It encourages a thoughtful and balanced approach to integrating technology into human interactions while recognizing its potential to shape society in various ways. Dutton and Graham also explore this by saying it is “difficult to escape the internet in a wide variety of areas” (2014), implying the internet has become almost ubiquitous. This could be due to factors like the integration of the internet into work, education, entertainment, and communication, making it not just a supplementary or optional feature but a critical component for various activities and services. By building our current society around technological features, we are becoming increasingly more dependent on it for our current survival.

Human needs and desires drive technological innovation, and technology, in turn, shapes human behaviour, culture, and society. Technology has developed its own behaviour norms, therefore can influence and even reshape the way people act and interact within particular situations or environments. Surry and Baker dive into this idea by stating, “Technology can change what behaviours are considered normal within a given situation” (2015). This quote brings to attention the dynamic relationship between technology and social norms. It highlights that technology has the potential to redefine what is considered normal behaviour within specific situations or context. For example, society has set a stereotype around video games to be a masculine activity, which has led to environments that are “often extremely toxic for women and minorities” (Nakamura, 2014). The language and actions that are performed online would never be accepted within ‘real life’ society, however online it is ignored and almost expected to occur. As a female gamer myself, I can confirm that when I log onto an online game, I am expected to be met with sexist comments. I have never felt that way while walking to a supermarket though and I think that perfectly highlights the divide between online and offline societal norms. As technology continues to advance, it will likely continue to influence and shape the behaviours and expectations of individuals and societies, possibly not for the better.

Is Media to Blame?
While we have examined the intrusive nature of media and its impact on society's evolution, a crucial question arises: to what extent can we attribute responsibility for our thoughts and actions to this technology? It is essential to recognize that technology itself did not autonomously evolve into the backbone of our society; instead, we have actively shaped and integrated it into our daily lives. We have bestowed upon it the power it wields, and, technically, we retain the agency to control its influence. The amount of time an individual spends on their phone, the data they consume, and the impact it has on their lives are ultimately personal choices. It is also essential to acknowledge that companies have learned to exploit the powerful impact of media, enabling them to 'directly manipulate our experience of the world' (Seymour, 2019). In this context, media serves as a tool, working in tandem with human psychology to control information, distort facts, and manipulate perceptions. As our society has evolved, we have become exposed to a greater volume of global media, creating the illusion that technological media forms are more influential and pervasive than their predecessors. However, as our understanding of human psychology has deepened, we have gained more tools to navigate and potentially mitigate the impact of media. Is media really as influential and all-encompassing as suggested by Chun, or is it just a tool that humans are exploiting?

Conclusion
In conclusion, our relationship with modern media is undeniably complex. While it has the power to shape our thoughts and habits, we cannot solely blame it for our actions. As we continue to evolve alongside advancing technology, it is essential to recognize the intricate interdependence between society and media. We must strive for a better understanding of how media affects us and who wields the power to influence it, as well as acknowledging the potential for positive impacts in education, information dissemination, and entertainment. This ongoing evolution will require a thoughtful, balanced approach to ensure that media continues to serve society's best interests.
