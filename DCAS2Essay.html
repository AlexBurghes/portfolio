Exploring Interactivity and User Agency in Media

1st Year, Semester 2 University Assignment
February 2023 - April 2023

Introduction
Interactivity has revolutionised the way we both consume and engage with media, giving users the ability to actively participate in the creation and distribution of online content. It has become an essential component of any and all media formats; video games, social media, mobile apps, websites, to name a few. This essay is going to dive into the concept of interactivity and further how it is allowing the user to be the author in interactive media, drawing on relevant theory and examples.

How do we Define Interactivity?
First, it is important to define what is meant by interactivity in the context of interactive media. According to Murray (1997), interactivity refers to “the degree to which the user can participate in modifying the form and content of a mediated environment in real time”. This definition emphasises the role of the user, and magnifies on the idea that they are shaping and modifying the content they interact with, rather than just consuming it passively. In essence, interactivity allows users to interact with media in a way that is meaningful to them, their interests, preferences and desires. It creates a communication channel between both the user and the medium, allowing users to have agency over their experience.

User Authorship
The concept of user authorship is integral to interactivity, as it allows users to actively participate in the creation and customisation of their content, hence blurring the lines between consumer and producer. Taking this idea into consideration, interactivity allows the user to become the authors of their experience and the media content they view online. As reported by Bruns (2008), interactivity represents a shift from a focus on production to a focus on “produsage”. Produsage is said to be a form of collaborative production where a user not only consumes content, but also actively produces content, either for themselves or for other users. This shift in focus from the industry from production to produsage emphasises the importance of user authorship in interactive media.

An example of interactivity mixing with user authorship is fan fiction. This refers to stories or theories written by fans of a particular franchise or series that are set within the world of that franchise. Fan fiction allows users to create their own stories and characters within an already existing narrative, hence continuing to blur the boundaries between producer and consumer. Fan fiction has allowed for increasingly growing communities to form online, which consist of users from all over the world, joining their ideas together to become a major part of the culture and fan base.

A further example of user authorship is video games. Video games allow users to actively participate in creation of the game’s narrative, with sometimes the action of the user directly consequencing the storyline. This allows the user to become the author of their own experience, shaping the story in a way that is unique to them. Furthermore, with video games becoming an increasingly more popular form of digital entertainment, users can collaborate online to share their experiences together, and subsequently, create infinite more possibilities that one individual could create alone.

The use of social media is yet another great example of user authorship. Being able to engage in real time with other users from across the globe, creating media content together and interacting with each other's creations. As a result, interactivity has revolutionised the way we, as a society, communicate and share information, with now the majority of users turning to social media as their primary form of news and entertainment.

User Empowerment
Interactivity has the potential to empower users, giving them the ability to engage with media in a way that is meaningful to them, specifically allowing them to control their media experiences. One example of this, can be seen in the way that social media platforms such as Twitter, are enabling users to create their own content and interact with others on the platform in real time. This power in interactivity lies in its ability to give users agency over their experiences. A key feature of video games is the sense of empowerment that they provide to users. Users can shape their experiences through the narrative in a way that feels personal and meaningful to them. As Gee (2003) notes, “the power of video games as learning environments lies in the face that they are highly interactive and give the player a great deal of control over his or her own learning.” Video games can be much more than just a source of entertainment; they can provide learning opportunities and areas of skill development. Educational games are designed to teach users about different subjects, for example, “Foldit” is a game which challenges the user to predict how certain proteins will fold, which in broad terms, can help with disease research. Another example would be “Minecraft: Education Edition”, which is a classroom focused version of “Minecraft”. It allows students to tour historical sites and do interactive science experiments for example. This new level of interactive experiences is particularly appealing to users who might feel disempowered, such as young people or users from marginalised communities. However, it is equally valid to address that not all video games are created for the cause of greater social impact. Some games, such as GTA, promote harmful stereotypes and/or promote violence and aggression. As users and developers, it is important to recognise the impact of video games on society and try to create impactful games that promote positive social change.

Digital Divide
The digital divide refers to the gap between those in the population who have access to technology and those who do not have access. Interactivity has the potential to begin to bridge that gap by empowering the user with the ability to create and distribute content. Noted by Fuchs (2014), “interactive media are not only sites of social interaction but also have the potential to enable users to take an active role in their social environment”. For example, modern social media platforms enable users to create their own content and connect with people across the world, regardless of their physical location or socioeconomic status, which even 10-20 years ago would have been a near impossible thought. However, it is important to note that although interactivity can promote collaboration and deliberation, it can also exacerbate existing inequalities, caused by some groups of the population to be “left behind” due to lack of access or resources. In a means to correct this problem, more recent technologies have been developed which are more accessible and user friendly, allowing groups of users who may feel excluded to be connected to the online world. Developers and producers are continuing to address the social and economic inequalities that contribute to the digital divide, and hopefully in another few years, we will be able to banish the thought of any excluded groups, allowing everyone to be able to collaborate and interact no matter the physical distance or economic differences.

Collective Intelligence
Collective intelligence refers to the idea that a group of individuals working together can create knowledge and solutions that are beyond the capabilities of one individual working alone. The use of interactivity and the ability now to collaborate on projects from across the world enables users to work together and share information, leading to the creation of a collective intelligence. As said by Levy (1997), “the digital medium fosters the creation of a collective intelligence that enables groups to solve problems that would be beyond the reach of any individual”. An example of this in action is Wikipedia, which was created and is maintained by a global community of contributors. Although there are many concerns about the accuracy of Wikipedia, it has been found in studies that it is generally just as accurate as traditional encyclopaedias (Giles,2005). The concept of collective intelligence also applies to social media platforms, which allow users to connect with one another on a global scale. Twitter has been used in the past to mobilise political movements, such as the Arab Spring protests in 2011, or, as another example, to coordinate disaster relief efforts after various natural disasters, this is becoming increasingly more popular after it has shown that it is effective (Palen at al., 2010). Overall, the potential for collective intelligence in relation to interactivity is immense, and it has the power to revolutionise the way we solve problems as a society.

Deliberation
Interactivity is a key component of deliberation and discussion, allowing the ability to engage with others in real time allows for the exchange of ideas and opinions, leading to a more diverse and informed public discourse. This is particularly relevant in the context of political discourse. Kligker-Vilenchik, Mihailidis, and Lewis (2017) note that “social media platforms have the potential to facilitate deliberative democracy by providing spaces for civic discussion and engagements.” This shows that interactivity can therefore promote a more democratic and inclusive public discourse. Virtual meeting spaces, such as video conferencing and virtual reality environments also offer new opportunities for deliberation in terms of interactive media. Having a platform that allows remote collaboration and communication, allows users from around the world to participate in shared decision-making processes. For example, recently virtual reality has been used in the urban planning industries and architecture, in order to effectively and efficiently plan out areas of land (Hirschberg et al., 2018).

Another example of deliberation in interactive media, is the use of online forums to facilitate public participation. These forums allow citizens to discuss and debate issues with each other, and with the policy makers themselves, with the target of obtaining a more informed and democratic decision. An example of this in action, would be the European Union, who has previously used online deliberation platforms such as Your Voice to engage citizens in debates about policy issues, ranging from climate change to immigration. This use of deliberation, in theory, allows users to highlight their opinion and concerns, hence helping to shape the way forward in society. Therefore, this use of participatory deliberation gives the user more empowerment and control over the world around them, suggesting that they indeed become more of the “author” of these policies and processes, rather than just a follower of them.

Privacy
The ability to share personal information while engaging with others in real time raises concerns about the privacy and data protection associated with interactivity. With the proliferation of digital devices and the internet, privacy issues have become increasingly more complex and therefore, difficult to navigate. A primary concern with privacy in interactive media is the collection and use of personal information by companies and organisations. In order to improve user experience, many websites and applications collect user data in order to personalise the content they see and give them a more unique viewing. However, in some cases this data can be sold to third party organisations, to be used for targeted advertising for example, this in turn leads to a higher chance of data breaches, information being stolen, which can possibly cause serious consequences for users.

In an effort to address these issues, many countries have implemented data protection laws and regulations, for example the European Union’s General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) and, in the United States, the California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA). These laws and regulations require companies to obtain user consent for data collection, and also provide clear information with how their data will be used, along with the option for users to access and delete the data upon their request. They are said to be some of the toughest privacy and security laws in the world, with those who violate their standards receiving penalties reaching into millions of euros/dollars.

Another aspect of privacy in interactive media is the use of encryption and other equal security measures to protect user data from unauthorised access. Encryption is a method of encoding data, which results in it only being able to be accessed and read by authorised parties. It is commonly used in online transactions and private communication. However, there is a debate about the balance between privacy and security, the argument is that although an overly strong encryption seems more private and secure, it can also make it difficult for law enforcement agencies to investigate crimes and protect public safety. Overall the issue of privacy in relation to interactive media is complex and multifaceted, as it requires careful consideration from both individual and societal needs. While data protection laws and encryption technologies do help mitigate the risks, in order to ensure that privacy concerns are adequately addressed in the design and use of interactive media, there needs to be an ongoing dialogue between users, organisations and policy makers.

Conclusion
In conclusion, the statement “interactivity is about the user also being an author” is highly relevant in the context of interactive media, as it emphasises the importance of user empowerment and participation in the design and use of digital technologies. Interactivity provides users with the capability to participate in the creation and distribution of content, hence blurring the lines between the producer and the consumer. It has the power to bridge together the social and economical gaps in the digital divide and to promote collective intelligence and deliberation. However, it also presents challenges, particularly around privacy and data protection. The ongoing development of interactive media presents infinite more opportunities, but also challenges, for the future of media and society. Overall, the statement “interactivity is about the user also being an author” highlights the importance of user agency and creativity in the digital age. As interactive media continues to evolve and expand, it is crucial that we remain attentive to all the uses and work together to build an inclusive, empowering and ethical digital future.
